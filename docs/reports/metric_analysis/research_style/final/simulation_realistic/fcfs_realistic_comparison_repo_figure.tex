\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The main analysis note used a deliberate stress-test configuration
($\lambda=100$ arrivals per day against $S=32$ slots, demand-to-capacity ratio
$3.1\mathord{:}1$) to expose how the simulation metrics behave under severe
capacity constraints. This supplementary note repeats the core comparison under
a more plausible, though still stylized, two-class parameterization: an
MRI-like diagnostic class and a behavioral-health follow-up class. The
comparison setting has $24$ daily arrivals, $20$ slots, a $28$-day booking
horizon, and class-specific behavioral parameters from the model formulation
note. Thirty independent seeds are used throughout.

The aggregate output improves sharply when demand pressure is reduced.
Average utilization rises to $0.934$ and overall served rate rises to $0.789$,
compared with $0.842$ and $0.269$ in the stress-test baseline. The more
important new result is the emergence of a large class-level access gap:
Class~1 served rate is $0.881$, while Class~2 served rate is $0.661$. Under the
current pooled FCFS formulation, this gap is mechanically consistent with
asymmetric behavioral parameters rather than explicit class priority. Class~1
has a higher delay tolerance, lower baseline no-show probability, and lower
cancellation hazard; Class~2 is disadvantaged along all three stages of the
access pipeline. Thus, the stress-test note explains the aggregate mechanics,
while this comparison shows that aggregate improvement can still conceal
substantial class-level divergence.
\end{abstract}

\section{What Changes and What Does Not}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Aggregate utilization and served rate.}
Both improve substantially. Utilization rises from $0.842$ to $0.934$; served
rate rises from $0.269$ to $0.789$. The primary driver is the lower demand
pressure combined with much lower cancellation hazards. With
$\phi=0.01$--$0.02$ per day instead of $0.10$ per day, far fewer booked slots
are vacated before service. For a mean delay near $10.9$ days, the probability
of avoiding cancellation is approximately $0.99^{10.9}\approx0.90$ for Class~1
and $0.98^{10.9}\approx0.80$ for Class~2, compared with
$0.9^{8.35}\approx0.42$ in the stress-test baseline. The served-rate gain is
also mechanically consistent with the lower demand-to-capacity ratio: the
nominal capacity ceiling is $20/24=0.833$, and the simulated served rate
($0.789$) is close to that bound after cancellations, no-shows, and balking.

\paragraph{Balking collapses; the offered/accepted gap nearly vanishes.}
In the stress-test baseline, mean offered wait is $9.30$ days, near the 9-day
balking threshold, which produces a $35.6\%$ balked share and a $0.95$-day gap
between offered and accepted wait. In the comparison parameterization, mean
offered wait is $10.87$ days, below the Class~1 threshold of 21 days and below
the Class~2 threshold of 14 days. Only $3.3\%$ of arrivals balk. Because few
high-delay patients are removed from the accepted sample, offered and accepted
wait are nearly identical ($0.02$ days apart). In this lower-load regime, the
selection problem in mean accepted wait is much smaller than in the stress-test
baseline.

\paragraph{No-show share is similar; the mechanism is different.}
No-show shares are similar ($5.2\%$ versus $5.6\%$ of arrivals), but for
different reasons. In the stress-test baseline, many accepted delays exceed the
6-day no-show threshold, so many booked patients face the high-delay no-show
probability $\xi^{\text{high}}=0.30$. In the comparison parameterization, the
21-day Class~1 threshold means that most accepted Class~1 bookings face only
$\xi^{\text{low}}_1=0.01$. The aggregate no-show share is instead pulled up by
Class~2, whose $\xi^{\text{low}}_2=0.15$ applies even for short-wait offers.
Under the current formulation, this makes Class~2 a persistent source of
booked-slot loss even when delays are moderate.

\paragraph{A large class access gap emerges.}
The most important new finding is the $22$-percentage-point class served-rate
gap shown in Table~\ref{tab:class-outcomes} and Figure~\ref{fig:class-served-gap}:
Class~1 served rate is $0.881$, while Class~2 served rate is $0.661$. This is
far larger than the near-zero gap at the symmetric stress-test baseline. The
horizontal class-level comparison makes this visible directly.

The gap is mechanically consistent with three compounding Class~1 advantages:
higher delay tolerance (balking threshold 21 rather than 14 days), lower
baseline no-show probability ($0.01$ rather than $0.15$), and lower daily
cancellation probability ($0.01$ rather than $0.02$). Under pooled FCFS, these
advantages act through the shared booking pool. Class~1 accepts more long-delay
offers, keeps more booked appointments, and loses fewer visits before service.
Class~2 is disadvantaged along the same stages. The output therefore suggests a
shared-capacity spillover rather than explicit class priority.

This finding is not recoverable from aggregate metrics alone. Average
utilization ($0.934$) and overall served rate ($0.789$) both look high. The
class gap only appears when class-level served rates are reported separately.

\paragraph{No-offer remains zero; the qualitative horizon dynamics hold.}
As in the stress-test baseline, no-offer is zero. This does not mean that the
system is unconstrained; it means that, under the current formulation,
cancellations and daily slot rollover are sufficient to prevent full horizon
saturation in the observed runs. This should be read together with the served
rate, cancellation share, and class-level served rates rather than interpreted
as evidence of unconstrained access.

\section{Summary}
%% ============================================================

The stress-test note showed that low served rate can be mechanically consistent
with high utilization when demand strongly exceeds capacity. This supplementary
comparison shows what changes under a lower-load, asymmetric two-class
parameterization. Aggregate outcomes improve: utilization rises to $0.934$ and
overall served rate rises to $0.789$. The loss-channel mix also changes:
balking falls sharply, cancellation losses are lower, and no-offer remains zero.

The critical remaining issue is class-level access. Under the current pooled
FCFS formulation, Class~1 reaches a served rate of $0.881$, while Class~2
reaches only $0.661$. This gap is mechanically consistent with asymmetric
behavioral parameters: Class~1 is more willing to accept delayed offers, less
likely to cancel, and less likely to no-show at low delays. The comparison
therefore does not contradict the stress-test note. It extends it: improving
aggregate performance does not necessarily remove class-level divergence when
patient classes differ in behavioral risk.
